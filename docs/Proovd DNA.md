\## 2. Border + border radius

The border is a 2px outside border on desktop and 1px outside on phone, usually \`#41ED98\` unless specified otherwise.

Border buttons are 2px border on laptop and 1px border on phone.

It should be applied tastefully to elements that are white on a white background to separate, for example a calendar or a small white section.

Border radius on desktop is 1px and on mobile it is 0.5px on basically everything — sharp.

\## 3. Font

Satoshi with all its weights used accordingly.

Satoshi is not a preference — it is part of the brand, and it must never fail silently:

\- Every page loads Satoshi. \*\*Self-hosted \`@font-face\` files shipped with the product are the required setup\*\* (they cannot be blocked or rate-limited); the Fontshare link is acceptable only for throwaway prototypes.

\- The system-font fallback stack exists only to keep text readable \*while\* Satoshi arrives — it is never an acceptable final state.

\- \*\*Verify the font actually loaded\*\*: after \`document.fonts.ready\` (with a timeout backstop), run \`document.fonts.check('16px Satoshi')\`. If it fails, surface a visible notice in the accent yellow treatment (1) — "Satoshi didn't load" — so the failure is caught and fixed, never quietly shipped.

\- No generator, AI or human, may substitute another typeface or decide the fallback "looks fine." If Satoshi isn't loading, that is a bug to surface, not a decision to make.

\- Text-in-motion splits only after fonts settle (6.4).

\## 4. Spacing and layout

Design pages as a sequence of distinct visual moments, not as a uniform stack of cards.

The layout should feel editorial, spacious, and intentionally composed. Use scale, whitespace, alignment, and position to communicate hierarchy before introducing borders, containers, or decorative UI.

\### 4.1 Create hierarchy through space

Spacing should reflect the relationship between elements:

\- Keep a heading close to the content it introduces.

\- Keep supporting text close to its related action.

\- Use substantially more space between unrelated sections than within a section.

\- Give important content more surrounding whitespace.

\- Avoid distributing elements at mechanically equal intervals.

As a general rule:

\- Related elements: \`4–24px\`

\- Elements within the same content group: \`24–40px\`

\- Separate sections: \`64–120px\`

\- Major page transitions: \`96–160px\`

Use these ranges as guidance rather than fixed values. Optical balance is more important than mathematical uniformity.

\### 4.2 Avoid the default card stack

Do not place every piece of content inside an identical bordered container.

Use a card only when the content needs:

\- A clear interactive boundary

\- A distinct state

\- Separation from its surrounding context

\- A grouped set of controls

\- Emphasis as the primary focal point

Allow secondary content to sit directly on the page. When every section is boxed, no section feels important.

\### 4.3 Establish one focal point at a time

Each viewport should have one visually dominant element.

Create emphasis using a combination of:

\- Larger scale

\- Increased whitespace

\- Stronger contrast

\- A filled background

\- A distinctive shape

\- A wider or narrower composition

Supporting elements should remain quieter and should not compete with the primary focal point.

\### 4.4 Use varied composition

Avoid repeating the same width, alignment, and structure for every section.

Create rhythm by varying:

\- Content width

\- Horizontal alignment

\- Text and graphic placement

\- Filled and outlined elements

\- Dense and open areas

\- Large and small typographic moments

Asymmetry is encouraged, but the page should still feel visually balanced by aligning to sides and overall visual balance.

\### 4.5 Use a consistent spacing scale

Prefer values from the shared spacing scale:

\`4, 8, 12, 16, 24, 32, 48, 64, 80, 96, 120, 160\`

Do not introduce arbitrary spacing values unless optical correction requires them.

\### 4.6 Treat whitespace as an active element

Empty space is not unused space. It should:

\- Separate stages in the user journey

\- Reduce perceived complexity

\- Draw attention toward important content

\- Create pacing as the user moves down the page

\- Make actions feel deliberate rather than crowded

Do not fill empty areas simply to make the layout appear more efficient.

\### 4.7 Keep actions attached to their context

Buttons and controls should remain visually connected to the content they act on.

\- Place primary actions close to their explanatory copy.

\- Avoid separating a button from its context with excessive space.

\- Use larger gaps after the complete content-and-action group.

\- Do not place several unrelated actions in a visually identical row or stack.

\### 4.8 Prefer visual grouping over explicit boundaries

Group content first through:

1\. Proximity

2\. Alignment

3\. Typography

4\. Background contrast

5\. Borders or containers

Borders should be the last grouping tool, not the default one.

\### 4.9 Preserve rhythm on smaller screens

On mobile:

\- Retain meaningful whitespace rather than compressing every section.

\- Reduce large desktop gaps proportionally, not completely.

\- Stack asymmetric compositions without turning them into identical cards.

\- Keep text widths comfortable and avoid full-width paragraphs where possible.

\- Ensure each screenful still has a clear focal point.

\### Do

\- Use generous gaps between distinct stages.

\- Let important elements occupy more visual space.

\- Vary section composition while preserving alignment logic.

\- Allow content to sit directly on the page.

\- Use typography and whitespace to establish hierarchy.

\- Keep related text, controls, and feedback visually close.

\### Avoid

\- Repeating the same card for every content item.

\- Giving all sections equal visual weight.

\- Using borders to solve every grouping problem.

\- Applying identical vertical gaps throughout the page.

\- Filling every empty area with content or decoration.

\- Centering or aligning everything in the same way.

\- Making decorative elements compete with functional content.

\### Review checklist

Before approving a layout, confirm that:

\- The primary focal point is immediately clear.

\- Related elements appear as a group without requiring a border.

\- Major sections have noticeably more separation than internal elements.

\- The page contains variation in scale, width, or alignment.

\- Cards are used intentionally rather than by default.

\- Whitespace creates pacing instead of feeling accidental.

\- Each action is clearly associated with the content it affects.

\- The layout still feels composed when viewed one screenful at a time.

\## 5. UX — The Filter

This section is a filter, not a layout. Run any surface through it — a terms-of-service page, a settings form, a dense operational dashboard — and it comes out simple, alive, and compulsively usable while keeping every feature it started with.

It makes no assumptions about what the product currently is. It must never be updated to reference specific features, and it never mandates a specific format (cards, swipes, feeds). It describes how any surface should \*behave\*, forever.

\*\*The premise:\*\* an interface is not a place the user looks at. It is a conversation the user is inside of. A good conversation says one thing at a time, responds to what you do, remembers where you left off, and ends — leaving you wanting the next one. Rebuild every surface as a short sequence of moments around one loop.

Complexity is never deleted. It is staged. The measure of success is not how little the product does — it is how little the user must hold in their head at any moment.

\### 5.1 The One-Question Rule

Every moment (one screenful, one focused view) exists to answer exactly one of the user's questions:

\- "What's happening?" (status)

\- "What should I do?" (action)

\- "How am I doing?" (progress)

\- "What is this?" (understanding)

Rules:

\- Identify the question before designing the moment. Write it down. If it can't be stated in one sentence, the moment doesn't exist yet.

\- If a moment answers two questions, split it into two moments.

\- The answer must be readable in under three seconds, without scanning.

\- Everything in the moment either answers the question or points to the next one. Anything else moves down an altitude (5.2).

This rule is where zero cognitive friction comes from. Friction is not too many pixels — it is too many simultaneous questions.

\### 5.2 The Three Altitudes

Every feature, control, and data point in the product is sorted into exactly one of three altitudes. Nothing is ever removed — everything is staged.

1\. \*\*Glance\*\* — what changed and what state things are in. Readable in three seconds: one hero element (5.6) plus a quiet line or two of context.

2\. \*\*Act\*\* — the single next-best action, already chosen by the product. One primary control, attached to the copy that explains it.

3\. \*\*Explore\*\* — the full control surface: every setting, table, filter, history, and edge-case action. Complete, dense if it needs to be, and exactly one intentional gesture away (a tap, a drawer, a link) — never buried two levels deep, never mixed into Glance or Act.

Rules:

\- Glance + Act is the default view of any surface. Explore is always reachable and never the landing state.

\- The product does the ranking. Never present a menu of equal options — that transfers the thinking cost to the user. If everything looks important, the product has failed to decide.

\- Moving something to Explore is not hiding it. Explore is a first-class, well-designed space (tab drawers on \`#012D10\`, section modes, the full spacing system apply) — it is where power users live.

\- Unsure whether something is Act or Explore? Ask: would a first-week user need it today? If not, Explore.

This is how a surface keeps the full depth of a dashboard while landing like a single sentence.

\### 5.3 The Loop, not the Map

A dashboard is a map: everything visible at once, all equal weight, the user does the thinking. An experience is a loop:

\*\*arrive → see what changed → do one thing → see the consequence → reach closure → leave with a reason to return\*\*

Rules:

\- Design the loop before any screen. Name the loop's verb — the one thing a user does here repeatedly (decide, review, publish, respond, check).

\- Every moment is a stop on the loop. Every feature is reachable from inside the loop (through Explore), never as a flat menu of destinations.

\- Show the consequence of every action immediately and concretely ("Sent. 3 left."), in the same moment or the next. The closed feedback loop is what makes a product feel alive under the user's hands.

\- A surface with nothing to do right now is still a loop stop: it shows what the system is doing on the user's behalf (5.5) and when to come back.

\### 5.4 Finite by design

No endless anything. Sessions end on purpose, and the ending is the reward.

Rules:

\- Every session has a designed terminal state: a real "you're done" moment — hero-scale, worth the delight budget (5.8) — never an empty state or a dead end.

\- Closure always ships with anticipation: the done-moment states, concretely, what will exist when the user returns. Specific times and numbers ("lands at 9pm", "3 new by Friday") beat vague promises.

\- Deal work in finite, countable units — a set to review, a number to clear — with the count visibly going down. Zero means the session is over.

\- Never manufacture items to extend a session. Never use infinite feeds, autoplay-next, or bottomless lists. Genuinely long content (tables, history) lives at Explore and paginates honestly.

The count going down and the earned ending are the compulsion mechanics — the user returns because finishing felt good, not because leaving was made hard.

\### 5.5 The system is alive

The strongest reason to return is knowing something is happening while you're away.

Rules:

\- Whenever the product is working on the user's behalf, say so at Glance, in present-tense verbs ("Scanning…", "Processing…", "Watching for replies").

\- Pair every live verb with a horizon: what will happen, and roughly when.

\- Give liveness one consistent look everywhere: the selection/loading treatment (\`#41ED98\` at 5% + \`#41ED98\` border).

\- Anticipation replaces notification pressure. Because arriving always shows what changed since last time, the product never needs to beg.

\- Never fake liveness. A live verb must correspond to a real process.

\### 5.6 One hero per moment

\- Every moment has exactly one element at display scale — usually a number, a verb, or a short sentence. It is the answer to the moment's question.

\- Everything else whispers: smaller, quieter color per the section modes, more whitespace.

\- The hero is data or consequence, never decoration. A big number the user caused to change is the most addictive object in a product.

\- This is the UX extension of the focal-point rule (4.3): one focal point per viewport, chosen by meaning, not by style.

\### 5.7 Words are the interface

\- Write sentences, not labels. "2 minutes now, so nothing ever holds your money" beats "Payment setup".

\- Talk like a sharp friend: direct, concrete, a little playful, zero corporate padding.

\- Copy carries the context so chrome doesn't have to. A well-written sentence replaces an icon, a tooltip, and a help page.

\- Buttons say what happens, in the user's words ("Draft it", "Copy link") — never generic ("Submit", "OK").

\- Numbers do the showing: "4 waiting" beats "items pending review".

\- Legal, settings, and system text use the same voice at lower volume: plain language leads, the formal text sits at Explore.

\*\*Copy economy — say only what is necessary:\*\*

Default AI generation over-writes. It spoon-feeds: helper lines under every control, subtitles that restate the button, "Here you can…" introductions, welcome padding, explanations of things the design already makes obvious. All of it is noise. Good UX is intuitive first — words are the fallback, not the crutch.

\- The deletion test: remove the line. If nothing became harder to do or understand, it stays deleted.

\- Every line must either change what the user does or be necessary to understanding. There is no third reason for copy to exist.

\- If a moment needs a sentence of instructions to be usable, the moment is designed wrong. Redesign it; don't caption it.

\- Never restate what an element already communicates — no subtitle repeating the button, no label repeating the heading, no confirmation repeating the action.

\- The two rules together: few words, but the words that remain have voice. One sharp sentence, not three polite ones.

\### 5.8 Friction and delight budgets

\*\*Friction budget\*\* — every tap must visibly move the loop forward:

\- A tap that only reveals more choices is spent friction. Collapse it.

\- Defaults do the work: the product pre-decides everything it can and lets the user correct, instead of asking first.

\- Never ask for input the product already knows or can infer.

\- Forms are flows, never walls of fields: one question per screen, dealt as a sequence (5.9).

\*\*Delight budget\*\* — one delight per moment, maximum:

\- A sticker (placeholder system), an oversized numeral, a satisfying state change — pick one.

\- Delight is earned, attached to progress: completing, unlocking, hitting zero. Unearned decoration is noise.

\- The done-moment (5.4) is where the budget spends biggest.

\### 5.9 Flow — one thing per screen

A dense screen makes the user do the sequencing. A flow does it for them.

When a surface asks for multiple inputs, decisions, or actions — a form, a setup, a review queue, a configuration — do not present it as one screen of stacked fields. Deal it as a sequence: \*\*one input, one decision, or one action per screen, animating from one to the next\*\*, so it feels like a single surface unfolding rather than separate pages.

This is the general move, not just a form pattern: whenever splitting a surface into a flowing sequence reduces what the user must hold in their head, split it. Each step becomes a moment obeying the One-Question Rule (5.1) — one question, one hero, one control.

Rules:

\- One thing per screen: the current input or decision is the hero; everything else waits its turn.

\- Animate the transition. Motion in one consistent direction carries context between steps and makes the sequence read as one continuous surface, not navigation.

\- The flow is finite and visible (5.4): show how many steps remain, and let the count go down. Completing the flow hits a done-moment.

\- Everything stays accessible — this is the hard constraint. Back always works, an overview of every step sits one gesture away (5.2, Explore), and any step can be returned to. A flow is a staged path through the surface, never a maze of 20 screens you can't get back to.

\- Advance with momentum: when an answer is unambiguous and safe, move to the next step automatically; when stakes are high, confirm. Each step should follow naturally from the last answer.

\- End with a summary moment: everything the user entered or decided, in one glance, editable — then closure.

\- Do not fragment for its own sake. Content consumed in a single glance stays together; splitting two trivial fields into two screens adds friction instead of removing it. The test is always cognitive load, not screen count.

\### 5.10 Guardrails — addictive, not manipulative

The pull of this system comes from momentum, closure, and anticipation. Never from:

\- Infinite feeds or autoplay

\- Guilt mechanics, shame copy, or loss-framing pressure

\- Fake urgency, fake scarcity, fake progress

\- Hidden exits, buried cancel/delete, or confirm-shaming

\- Notification spam

If a pattern makes the user feel worse for leaving, it's out. The user should leave feeling finished and return feeling curious.

\### 5.11 The one-shot recipe

To apply this filter to any surface — new or existing, trivial or dense — execute in order:

1\. \*\*Write the question.\*\* One sentence: what does the user want to know or do here? (5.1)

2\. \*\*Inventory everything.\*\* List every feature, control, and datum the surface must support. Nothing gets dropped.

3\. \*\*Sort into altitudes.\*\* Every item goes to Glance, Act, or Explore. (5.2)

4\. \*\*Name the loop.\*\* The verb, the consequence, the closure, the reason to return. (5.3, 5.4)

5\. \*\*Pick the hero.\*\* The one display-scale element that answers the question. (5.6)

6\. \*\*Write the words first.\*\* Draft the actual sentences before drawing anything, then run the deletion test on every line. (5.7)

7\. \*\*Deal the flow.\*\* If the surface collects inputs or decisions, sequence it one thing per screen, animated, finite, fully navigable. (5.9)

8\. \*\*Compose the moments.\*\* Apply Spacing and layout (4) for composition and Color (1) for section modes; live states use the selection treatment (5.5); spend the delight budget (5.8); animate everything per Motion (6).

9\. \*\*Run the checklist.\*\* (below)

\*\*Worked example — a terms-of-service page.\*\* Question: "What am I agreeing to?" Hero: "The short version:" followed by five plain-language points (Glance). Act: one button — "Sounds fair — continue". Explore: the complete legal text, one tap below, honestly formatted. Closure: "That's everything. You're covered." Nothing is simplified away — the full text is all there, staged.

\### 5.12 Comfort — the user is never lost

An interface is comfortable when three things are always known without thinking: where you are, how to get back, and what a tap will cost. Comfort is what makes depth explorable — users only open drawers they trust they can close.

Rules:

\- \*\*Wayfinding is ambient.\*\* The current location and the way back are readable at every scroll depth and every flow step. The menu — the way to anywhere — is one gesture from everywhere, always: never lost by scrolling, never buried behind a state, never present on some screens and missing on others.

\- \*\*Dense reference surfaces lead with the gist.\*\* Any long or dense document — legal text, terms, settings indexes, histories, documentation — opens with a plain-language overview at Glance: the short version first, the complete text one gesture below, honestly formatted (5.7). The user never mines a wall for what it means.

\- \*\*Forgiveness over interrogation.\*\* Prefer undo to "are you sure": reversible actions act immediately and confirm with their consequence (5.3); only genuinely destructive ones get a single calm confirmation. A curious tap never costs anything — that freedom is what makes Explore work.

\- \*\*The surface holds still.\*\* Space is reserved before content arrives (5.5), so the page never reflows under an incoming finger. Nothing moves under a tap that's already in flight.

\- \*\*Position survives.\*\* Scroll depth, entered text, and flow progress persist through interruption. Coming back never means starting over.

\### 5.13 Cognitive load — one slot of working memory

Cognitive load is not visual density; it is how many things the user must hold in their head at once. The budget is one — the moment's question (5.1). Everything else is the product's job to carry.

Rules:

\- \*\*Recognition over recall.\*\* Never require remembering a value, a name, or a state from a previous screen — carry it forward and show it where it's needed.

\- \*\*Conclusions, not raw material.\*\* The product counts, compares, and states what it found ("3 need you today"); the underlying data sits at Explore for whoever wants it.

\- \*\*State the state.\*\* The active filter, mode, and context are always visible. If the user must remember how a view is configured, the view is wrong.

\- \*\*One vocabulary.\*\* Each thing has exactly one name, used identically in every moment, button, and toast. Synonyms are load.

\- \*\*Chunk everything countable.\*\* Deal work in graspable sets (5.4) and multi-part input as flows (5.9) — never one undifferentiated wall.

\- \*\*One new concept per moment.\*\* Introduce ideas exactly when they're needed — never in advance, never two at once.

\### 5.14 Staged, never amputated

Reducing cognitive load and friction \*\*never means removing features or shrinking what the product does.\*\* Cutting capability is not simplification — it is a smaller product wearing the same name. The craft — the entire point of this filter — is figuring out how to display complexity in a way that is simple and delightful.

Rules:

\- \*\*Full inventory, always.\*\* Every feature, control, and datum the surface supported still exists at some altitude (5.2). If something was dropped to make a screen calmer, the design failed — put it back and stage it.

\- \*\*Simplicity is a display problem, not a scope problem.\*\* Summarize it (Glance), pre-decide it (Act), sequence it (5.9), and craft the deep end (Explore) as carefully as the landing.

\- \*\*Delight keeps depth inviting.\*\* Staging earns the delight budget (5.8): the drawer that opens beautifully, the count that rolls down, the done-moment. Complexity displayed this way is not endured — it's enjoyed.

\- \*\*The two-user test.\*\* A power user can still do everything; a first-week user is never confronted with it. Both must be true at once — if either fails, the staging is wrong.

\### Do

\- State the moment's one question before designing it.

\- Let the product pre-rank and pre-decide; users correct, they don't configure.

\- End sessions on purpose, with a designed done-moment.

\- Show live work in present-tense verbs with a horizon.

\- Keep the full feature set at Explore — well-crafted and one gesture away.

\- Write the sentences before drawing the screens — then delete every line that changes nothing.

\- Deal multi-input surfaces as animated one-thing-per-screen flows, with every step reachable.

\- Keep the menu one gesture away from everywhere — wayfinding is ambient, never earned.

\- Lead dense reference content with its plain-language overview; stage the full version, never cut it.

\### Avoid

\- Landing users on a map of equal options.

\- Any list without an end.

\- Removing features to fake simplicity.

\- More than one question, one hero, or one delight per moment.

\- Asking users anything the product can infer.

\- Any mechanic that punishes leaving.

\- Spoon-feeding: helper lines, restated buttons, "Here you can…" filler, or any copy the design already makes obvious.

\- Walls of fields — and equally, fragmenting glanceable content into needless steps.

\### Review checklist

Before approving any surface, confirm:

\- The moment's one question is stated, and answered within three seconds.

\- Every feature of the surface exists at some altitude — nothing was dropped to achieve simplicity.

\- The next-best action is chosen by the product and attached to its context.

\- The loop closes: action → visible consequence → closure → stated reason to return.

\- Sessions are finite, and "done" is a designed moment.

\- Everything the system does in the background is visible as a live, truthful verb.

\- One hero and at most one delight per moment.

\- Copy reads like a sharp friend and every button says what happens.

\- Every line of copy survives the deletion test — nothing is spoon-fed.

\- Multi-input surfaces flow one thing per screen, with visible progress and every step reachable.

\- Nothing manipulates: no infinite feeds, no guilt, no fake urgency.

\- The user can say where they are and how to get back from any depth; the menu is one gesture away.

\- Dense reference content opens with the gist; the complete version is staged and reachable.

\- A first-time user could say what to do here without instructions.

\## 6. Motion

Motion is information, not decoration. It is how the interface proves it's alive (5.5), how state changes explain themselves so copy doesn't have to (5.7), and how flows feel like one surface unfolding (5.9).

\*\*GSAP is the motion runtime — non-negotiable.\*\* Core plus \*\*Flip\*\* (layout morphs), \*\*ScrollTrigger\*\* (scroll reveals), and \*\*SplitText\*\* (type in motion), with ScrambleText and TextPlugin where the kit calls for them. As of GSAP 3.13 the entire plugin set is free. No generator, AI or human, may reimplement Proovd motion in CSS transitions/animations, the Web Animations API, or another library — not as a fallback, not as a simplification, not because GSAP "wasn't available." If GSAP isn't available, the fix is to load it — \*\*exactly the way 6.7 says, no other way.\*\* A motion layer that fails must never leave a blank or frozen screen — and must never fail silently either (see 6.6).

\*\*The premise: nothing teleports.\*\* Every element that changes — size, place, state, screen — visibly travels from where it was to where it's going. If something appears from nowhere or vanishes without lineage, the motion system has failed. The interface is a physical material: it moves, resizes, and stretches under the user's touch.

\### 6.1 Motion tokens

One shared scale. All animation in the product draws from this object — never invent ad-hoc durations or eases.

\`\`\`js

export const MOTION = {

  dur: {

    instant: 0.12, // hover, press, focus response

    quick:   0.20, // small state changes, color swaps

    base:    0.35, // morphs, selection, most component motion

    slow:    0.60, // drawers, flow steps, large reveals

    grand:   0.90, // splash, page transitions — nothing exceeds this

  },

  ease: {

    out:    'power3.out',    // default — everything entering or expanding

    hero:   'power4.out',    // hero/text entrances — a sharper arrival than \`out\`

    move:   'power2.inOut',  // position changes, flow steps, page transitions

    snap:   'back.out(1.4)', // delight only: done-moments, stickers, toasts

    bounce: 'bounce.out',    // physical delight: progress landings, playful transitions

    exit:   'power2.in',     // everything leaving or shrinking

  },

  stagger: { tight: 0.04, base: 0.08 },

  text: { chars: 0.015, words: 0.065, lines: 0.08 }, // stagger for split-text reveals (6.4)

  dist: { enter: 16 }, // px; entrances travel 12–24px, never big slides

};

gsap.defaults({ ease: MOTION.ease.out, duration: MOTION.dur.base, overwrite: 'auto' });

\`\`\`

Rules:

\- On mobile, multiply durations by 0.85 — small screens read motion faster.

\- Interaction feedback (hover, press, focus) always answers within \`instant\`. Slower than that reads as lag, not motion.

\- \`snap\` and \`bounce\` are the delight eases. They spend the delight budget (5.8): at most one per moment, attached to progress. \`snap\` overshoots and settles (toasts, done-checks); \`bounce\` lands physically (progress fills, celebration transitions).

\- \`hero\` is reserved for the one hero entrance of a moment — usually its headline. Everything else enters with \`out\`.

\- \`exit\` is always faster than or equal to the entrance it undoes. Leaving is never slower than arriving.

\### 6.2 The Morph Principle

State changes are morphs, not swaps. The element the user touched is the element that transforms — it keeps its identity while changing shape.

\- A textbox, on focus, expands: it grows in height or width, its border animates to \`#41ED98\`, and the rest of the moment breathes aside to make room.

\- A card, on tap, grows into the panel it was hiding — the card \*is\* the panel, earlier.

\- A button, on submit, stretches into the progress bar (selection treatment, 1), which fills, then snaps into the done state. One element, three lives.

Implementation — GSAP \*\*Flip\*\* is the standard for every layout change:

\`\`\`js

const state = Flip.getState(el);      // 1. capture

el.classList.add('expanded');         // 2. change the DOM to the end state

Flip.from(state, {                    // 3. animate the difference

  duration: MOTION.dur.base, ease: MOTION.ease.out, absolute: true,

});

\`\`\`

Rules:

\- Origin-aware: things grow from where the user touched, not from the screen center. Set \`transformOrigin\` to the interaction point.

\- Prefer stretch and resize over fade. Fade is reserved for content with no previous position (freshly loaded data, first paint).

\- When a morph reveals new inner content, the container morphs first and the content fades/staggers in behind it (\`stagger.tight\`), never the reverse.

\- Anything that can be expressed as a morph must not be expressed as a cross-fade or a hard swap.

\### 6.3 The five tiers

Smallest to largest. Every tier uses the same tokens, so the whole product moves like one material.

\*\*1. Micro — hover, press, focus (\`instant\`):\*\*

\- Hover animates the variant swap already defined in Color (1) — background, text, and border tween to the hover colors, plus scale: \`1.3\` for the variant swap, \`1.02\` max for everything else. Nothing jumps between color states.

\- Press: scale to ~0.78, back on release. Every pressable element compresses — the material gives.

\- Focus: the border tweens to \`#41ED98\` and the element begins its morph (6.2).

\*\*2. State — selection, toggles, progress (\`quick\`–\`base\`):\*\*

\- The selection treatment (\`#41ED98\` at 5% + border) animates in — tint fades up, border color tweens, checkmark draws in with a stroke animation.

\- Progress bars fill with \`move\`; the fill is continuous, never stepped. Fills animate \`scaleX\` from a left origin — never \`width\`, which forces layout every frame.

\- Counts animate through their values (4 → 3 → 2 rolls, it doesn't blink).

\*\*3. Component — drawers, toasts, accordions, modals (\`base\`–\`slow\`):\*\*

\- Tab drawers (\`#012D10\`) slide in with \`out\`, slide away with \`exit\`; active/inactive text colors tween.

\- Toasts (accents, 1) enter with \`snap\` — they land, slightly overshoot, settle. They leave quietly with \`exit\`.

\- Accordions morph height (Flip or height tween) — never \`display: none\` jump-cuts. The 1px \`#41ED98\` divider draws in.

\- Modals and panels morph out of whatever summoned them (6.2), never fade in from a dimmed void.

\*\*4. Flow and page transitions (\`slow\`–\`grand\`, ease \`move\`):\*\*

\- Flow steps (5.9) move in one consistent direction; the outgoing step exits as the incoming enters, overlapping — one surface unfolding, not slides in a deck.

\- Shared-element handoff: if an element persists across two screens (a title, a number, a sticker), Flip it between them instead of animating it out and in.

\- Page transitions are one composed movement, under \`grand\`, and interruptible. Never scroll-jack; ScrollTrigger is for section reveals only — one reveal per section, elements entering by \`dist.enter\` with \`stagger.base\`.

\*\*5. Splash and loading (\`grand\`, once):\*\*

\- The splash is a single brand moment: placeholder stickers (1) assembling into place, then handing off to the first screen via shared-element morph. It plays once — never re-shown within a session, skipped entirely on return visits.

\- Loading states use the selection treatment (5.5) with a live verb. If a wait is under \`quick\`, show nothing — flashing a spinner for 100ms is noise.

\### 6.4 Type in motion

Type is the loudest element on most Proovd surfaces (5.6, 5.7), so it earns the most considered entrance. Text arrives in pieces — the headline assembles itself instead of simply being there. Built with GSAP \*\*SplitText\*\*.

\- \*\*Grain follows type role\*\* — what looks good as a title does not look good as a line. Display headlines reveal by \*\*word\*\*; sentence-length text (ledes, statements, multi-line intros) reveals by \*\*whole line\*\*; char-grain is a special effect for a wordmark or a single short hero word, not a default. Never char-split a full sentence.

\- \*\*Mask the reveal — this is the default.\*\* Wrap split lines in \`overflow: hidden\` line boxes (SplitText's \`mask: 'lines'\`) so pieces rise from behind a clean edge, not out of thin air. This is the type-level expression of "nothing teleports," and with the padding rule below it is validated on real Proovd type.

\- \*\*Give masks breathing room.\*\* An \`overflow: hidden\` line box crops ascenders and descenders (g, y, p) at rest. Every mask wrapper gets \`padding-block: 0.15em\` with a matching negative \`margin-block\` to cancel the layout shift. The one exception: display type with very tight leading (line-height under ~1.1) still can't be masked cleanly — reveal it unmasked (rise + fade) instead.

\- \*\*One text hero per moment.\*\* The single most important line gets the headline rise; everything else uses a quieter line/word reveal or none. Do not animate every string on the screen — equal motion is no motion (6.6).

\- \*\*Reveal once, then leave it alone.\*\* Text animates on entrance or on scroll-in (one reveal per section via ScrollTrigger), never on a loop, never on every re-render. Looping or ticking text is noise.

\- \*\*Always revert the split.\*\* After the reveal completes, revert SplitText so the DOM returns to clean, selectable, reflowable text. Never ship the split spans permanently — they break selection, screen readers, and reflow.

\- \*\*Split after fonts load.\*\* Measure line breaks once the real typeface is ready (\`document.fonts.ready\`), with a timeout fallback so a blocked font never stalls the reveal.

\- \*\*Body copy is never animated\*\* beyond its container's own entrance. Reading text must be readable instantly.

\- \`ScrambleText\` and similar character effects are reserved for a single deliberate accent (a number settling, one status word) — never for anything the user needs to read immediately.

\- Under \`prefers-reduced-motion\`, skip splitting entirely and render the full text at once.

\*\*The Motion Lab (\`demo/lab.html\`)\*\* is the tuning environment for this whole section: every animation family — text, hover, press, morph, UI, page transitions, scroll, stagger, counters, splash — with live controls and a code exporter. Each exported block carries a \`/\* proovd-motion {...} \*/\` JSON header; pasting blocks back is the canonical way to propose changes to this system's defaults. The recipes in 6.5 were chosen this way.

\### 6.5 The kit — canonical recipes

These are the chosen, tuned defaults for every animation family — exported from the Motion Lab and approved. When building any surface, \*\*pick from this kit first\*\*; invent a new animation only with a reason, and tune it in the Lab so the export can be reviewed. Parameters are exact.

\*\*Text\*\* (all splits revert on complete; masks per 6.4):

\- \*\*Headline rise\*\* — masked \*\*words\*\*, \`yPercent: 120 → 0\`, \`0.65s hero (power4.out)\`, stagger \`0.065\`. \*Use for:\* the hero headline and section headings — display type, up to ~2 lines. The signature Proovd reveal.

\- \*\*Line flip\*\* — whole \*\*lines\*\*, \`rotationX: -90 → 0\` from the baseline (\`transformOrigin: 50% 100%\`), rise \`0.5em\`, \`0.35s power3.in\`, stagger \`0.03\`. \*Use for:\* sentence-length text — ledes, statements, multi-line intros. Word- or char-grain reads wrong at this length; whole lines read right.

\- \*\*Word slide\*\* — \*\*words\*\* from the left, \`x: -60 → 0\` + fade, \`0.55s power3.out\`, stagger \`0.04\`. \*Use for:\* kickers, labels, and secondary headings — quieter than the headline rise, same direction of travel.

\- \*\*Scramble\*\* — \`0.4s\`, linear, upper/lowercase pool. \*Use for:\* one live status word settling ("Matching…" → "Matched") or a number locking in. Never body copy, never more than one per moment.

\- \*\*Typewriter\*\* — \`0.7s\`, linear. \*Use for:\* the product speaking a single conversational line (a done-moment quip, an empty-state greeting). One line, once.

\*\*Hover\*\* (respond within \`instant\`; color pairs from Section 1):

\- \*\*Variant swap\*\* — background/text tween to the opposite button variant (fill ↔️ \`2px\` border) \*\*plus scale to 1.3\*\*, \`0.15s power2.out\` in, \`0.2s\` out. \*Use for:\* THE primary CTA of a moment — it needs surrounding whitespace to breathe at that scale. One per moment.

\- \*\*Grow\*\* — scale to \`1.3\`, \`0.15s power2.out\`. \*Use for:\* stickers, icons, and compact tappables where a color swap would be noise.

\- \*\*Underline draw\*\* — \`scaleX: 0 → 1\` from the left on enter, collapse to the right on leave, \`0.3s power3.out\`. \*Use for:\* links, everywhere.

\- \*\*Fill sweep\*\* — brand fill sweeps \`scaleX\` left-to-right behind the label, \`0.3s power2.out\`, reverses right-to-left on leave. \*Use for:\* outline/secondary buttons.

\*\*Press:\*\*

\- \*\*Compress\*\* — scale to \`0.78\`, \`0.12s\` down, \`0.25s power3.out\` back. \*Use for:\* the default press on every button, card, and tappable — the material gives, deeply and playfully.

\- \*\*Squash & pop\*\* — \`scaleX: 1.06 / scaleY: 0.9\` down, release with \`back.out(2.5)\` over \`0.4s\`. \*Use for:\* delight presses — done-moment buttons, stickers. Spends the delight budget.

\*\*Morph\*\* (all via Flip, per 6.2):

\- \*\*Textbox expand\*\* — \`0.35s power3.out\`, border tweens to \`#41ED98\` on focus, \`#A2AFA8\` on blur. \*Use for:\* every text input.

\- \*\*Card → panel\*\* — container \`0.6s power3.out\`, revealed content staggers in at \`0.04\` behind the stretch. \*Use for:\* any summary that opens into its detail.

\- \*\*Button → progress → done\*\* — width morph \`0.35s\`, fill \`scaleX\` over \`1.2s power2.inOut\`, done-snap \`back.out(2)\`, return morph after \`1.2s\` hold. \*Use for:\* any submit that takes real time — publish, send, pay.

\- \*\*Tag → input\*\* — \`0.4s power3.out\`, focus lands in the input on complete. \*Use for:\* add-an-item affordances (goals, tags, filters).

\*\*UI components:\*\*

\- \*\*Toast\*\* — enters from below (\`y: 30\`) with \`back.out(1.7)\` over \`0.4s\`, holds \`~1s\`, exits down-fade \`0.2s power2.in\`. \*Use for:\* all confirmations; accent colors per Section 1.

\- \*\*Drawer\*\* — in \`0.4s power3.out\` with tabs staggering \`0.04\`; out \`0.28s power2.in\`. \*Use for:\* the Explore surface (5.2) and any side panel.

\- \*\*Accordion\*\* — height morph \`0.2s power3.out\`, chevron rotates 45°. \*Use for:\* FAQs and collapsible detail. Component state changes are \*\*quick\*\* — \`0.2s\` is the rule for accordion, modal, and tabs.

\- \*\*Modal from trigger\*\* — grows from the trigger's position (\`transformOrigin\` at the button), \`0.2s power3.out\`; scrim fades \`0.25s\`; exits \`0.2s power2.in\`. \*Use for:\* every modal — never fade in from a centered void.

\- \*\*Tab underline\*\* — \`Flip.fit\` between tabs, \`0.2s power3.out\`. \*Use for:\* all tab sets.

\- \*\*Dropdown\*\* — \`scaleY\` unfold from the top \`0.3s power3.out\`, items stagger \`0.04\`; exits \`0.15s power2.in\`. \*Use for:\* action menus.

\*\*Page transitions\*\* (overlap \`0.8\` — the incoming page is moving before the outgoing is gone):

\- \*\*Overlap slide\*\* — incoming page slides in over the outgoing (which retreats 35% and dims), \`0.7s\`. Two personalities: \*\*\`back.out(1.2)\`\*\* is the default (a confident landing with a hint of overshoot); \*\*\`bounce.out\`\*\* is for playful/celebration moments (level-ups, done-screens). Direction is semantic: \*\*left = forward\*\* in a flow, \*\*right = back\*\*, \*\*up = opening/ascending a level\*\*, \*\*down = dismissing/returning home\*\*. Pick direction by meaning, not variety.

\- \*\*Curtain\*\* — two \`#012D10\` panels close over the old page (\`0.55s power2.inOut\` each way), part to reveal the new. \*Use for:\* major chapter changes — entering a different product area.

\- \*\*Unroll\*\* — new page rises from the bottom (\`0.7s power3.out\`) while the old recedes (\`scale 0.97\`, dims). \*Use for:\* detail sheets and drill-ins that keep their parent's context underneath.

\*\*Scroll\*\* (once per element, never scroll-jacked):

\- \*\*Editorial rise\*\* — \`y: 48 → 0\` + fade, \`1.2s power3.out\`. \*Use for:\* content blocks on marketing/editorial pages — deliberately slower and farther than UI motion; it reads as luxury at scroll pace.

\- \*\*Scale-in\*\* — \`scale: 0.92 → 1\` + fade, \`0.6s back.out(1.2)\`. \*Use for:\* cards, stat tiles, and stickers entering.

\*\*Stagger:\*\*

\- \*\*Grid assemble\*\* — cells \`scale: 0 → 1\`, \`0.75s back.out(1.7)\`, stagger \`{each: 0.07, from: 'center', grid: 'auto'}\`. \*Use for:\* sticker walls, galleries, any grid arriving as one object.

\*\*Counter & progress:\*\*

\- \*\*Number roll\*\* — value tweens with \`snap\`, \`0.65s power2.out\`. \*Use for:\* every stat that changes — numbers travel, they don't blink.

\- \*\*Progress fill\*\* — \`scaleX: 0 → 1\`, \`0.95s bounce.out\`. \*Use for:\* determinate progress — the fill physically lands. Spends the delight budget.

\- \*\*Check draw\*\* — SVG stroke draws via dashoffset, \`0.6s power3.out\`. \*Use for:\* selection checkmarks and done-states.

\*\*Splash\*\* (once per session, exits upward):

\- \*\*Sticker assemble\*\* — stickers snap in (\`0.4s back.out(1.7)\`, stagger \`0.08\`), wordmark fades, holds \`0.5s\`, exits up \`0.7s power2.inOut\`. \*Use for:\* the primary app splash.

\- \*\*Wordmark reveal\*\* — wordmark chars rise (\`0.5s power3.out\`, stagger \`0.04\`), then the same exit. \*Use for:\* minimal contexts — auth screens, embeds.

\### 6.6 Integrity rules

\- Animate \`transform\` and \`opacity\` only. Flip exists precisely so layout changes become transforms. Never tween \`width\`/\`height\`/\`top\`/\`left\` directly except inside a Flip.

\- Motion never blocks input. Every animation is interruptible (\`overwrite: 'auto'\`); the user can act mid-animation and the motion follows them. No "wait for the animation" moments, ever.

\- One hero motion per moment (5.6). The focal element gets the composed animation; everything else whispers — small distances, tight staggers, no ease louder than \`out\`.

\- Motion must explain one of: origin, destination, relationship, or state change. Motion that explains nothing is decoration and spends the delight budget (5.8) — one per moment, earned by progress.

\- \`prefers-reduced-motion: reduce\` collapses everything to \`quick\` opacity fades. The product stays fully usable with motion off.

\- Timelines, not scattered tweens: each moment's motion is one \`gsap.timeline()\` so it can be composed, interrupted, and reversed as a unit.

\- \*\*Content is never hidden behind motion that might not run.\*\* Anything the user must see (a splash overlay, a to-be-revealed section, split text) has a guaranteed exit: guard for a missing/failed motion layer, wrap init so a throw still reveals content, and keep a timeout backstop that clears any covering overlay. A frozen screen is worse than no animation. This is why the runtime ships vendored or built-in, never fetched from elsewhere (6.7).

\- \*\*Fail loud, not silent.\*\* GSAP and Satoshi are load-bearing. If the motion runtime is missing or throws, or Satoshi fails \`document.fonts.check\` (3), the page must show a visible notice — accent yellow (1), plain words: "Motion failed to load" / "Satoshi didn't load" — alongside the graceful degrade. A quiet downgrade that nobody notices is how the brand erodes one deploy at a time.

\### 6.7 Shipping GSAP — write tags, never library code

A generator must \*\*never attempt to write out, reproduce, or "inline" GSAP source itself\*\* — a language model cannot emit 150KB of minified library code; the attempt produces corruption or a silent retreat to CSS animation, both forbidden. Including GSAP is mechanical, and there are exactly two ways:

\*\*1. The standard way — reference the vendored files.\*\* The repo ships the full runtime in \`demo/vendor/\` (\`gsap.min.js\`, \`Flip.min.js\`, \`ScrollTrigger.min.js\`, \`SplitText.min.js\`, \`ScrambleTextPlugin.min.js\`, \`TextPlugin.min.js\`). Every generated page includes this boilerplate verbatim, before its own script, adjusting only the relative path and dropping plugins it doesn't use:

\`\`\`html

</p><p class="slate-paragraph">  gsap.registerPlugin(Flip, ScrollTrigger, SplitText);</p><p class="slate-paragraph">  gsap.defaults({ ease: &#x27;power3.out&#x27;, duration: 0.35, overwrite: &#x27;auto&#x27;, force3D: true });</p><p class="slate-paragraph">

\`\`\`

\*\*2. The standalone way — run the committed build.\*\* When the deliverable must be a single self-contained file (a download, an artifact, an email-able demo), write the page with the same \`src\` tags as above, then run:

\`\`\`

node demo/build-inline.js

\`\`\`

The build replaces each vendor \`src\` tag (or a \`\`-style marker) with the file's contents and fails the build if any reference is left unresolved. The tool does the inlining — never a person, never a model.

Rules:

\- CDNs are not an option in production; the vendored files are the source of truth.

\- gsap.min.js always loads first; \`registerPlugin\` always runs before any animation code.

\- The fail-loud guard (6.6) stays in the page either way — so a wrong path announces itself instead of quietly shipping a dead page.

\- A page generated without one of these two inclusion patterns is wrong, regardless of how correct its animation code is.

\### Do

\- Pull every duration and ease from \`MOTION\` — one material, one physics.

\- Include GSAP per 6.7 — vendor \`src\` tags, or the standalone build.

\- Pick from the kit (6.5) before inventing a new animation; tune deviations in the Lab.

\- Morph the element the user touched; keep its identity across states.

\- Respond to every interaction within \`instant\`.

\- Overlap exits and entrances so screens feel continuous.

\- Let counts roll, checkmarks draw, and progress fill — make change watchable.

\- Reveal headlines in pieces with SplitText, then revert the split.

\- Ship the motion runtime self-contained, with a fail-safe that always clears the screen.

\- Collapse to fades under reduced motion.

\### Avoid

\- Elements appearing or disappearing with no lineage.

\- Cross-fades where a morph is possible.

\- Animating everything equally — equal motion is no motion.

\- Ease or duration values invented outside the tokens.

\- Reimplementing motion in CSS or another library — GSAP and its plugins are the only runtime.

\- Hand-writing or hand-inlining GSAP source, or loading it from a CDN — vendor tags or the build (6.7), nothing else.

\- Silent downgrades: missing GSAP or Satoshi without a visible notice.

\- Motion that makes the user wait, or blocks input.

\- Spinners for sub-200ms waits, splash screens that replay, scroll-jacking anywhere.

\- Animating body copy, looping text effects, or leaving SplitText spans in the DOM.

\- An overlay or reveal that can trap the user if the motion layer fails to load.

\### Review checklist

Before approving any animated surface, confirm:

\- Nothing teleports — every change of size, place, or state visibly travels.

\- Every animation explains origin, destination, relationship, or state.

\- Hover, press, and focus respond within \`instant\`.

\- Exactly one hero motion per moment; at most one \`snap\`.

\- All values come from the \`MOTION\` tokens.

\- Every animation is a kit recipe (6.5), or its deviation has a stated reason.

\- Layout changes go through Flip; only transforms and opacity are tweened.

\- Everything is interruptible; input is never blocked.

\- Exits are as fast or faster than entrances.

\- Headlines reveal in pieces; splits are reverted so text stays selectable and reflows.

\- The runtime is self-contained and fails safe — a missing motion layer still shows all content, never a frozen screen.

\- Motion runs on GSAP with the required plugins, and Satoshi is verified loaded — any failure of either surfaces a visible notice, never a silent downgrade.

\- GSAP is included per 6.7: vendor \`src\` tags or the committed inline build — no hand-written library code, no CDN.

\- Reduced motion collapses to quick fades, shows full text at once, and loses no functionality.

\## 7. Element identity — telling things apart

The failure mode this section kills: a screen of identical grey-bordered rectangles where a textbox, a toggle, a stepper, and a card all look like the same object, with one green button floating in the monotone. If two elements do different things, they must not wear the same clothes.

\*\*The rule: role = silhouette.\*\* Every interactive role has one signature treatment, and no two roles share one. The test is \*\*cover the copy\*\*: hide every word on the screen — you must still be able to point at what's typeable, what's tappable, what's on/off, and what's just information. If you can't, the screen is wrong regardless of how clean it looks.

\### 7.1 The identity table

One treatment per role. These are exact.

\- \*\*Text input / textarea\*\* — white fill, \`#A2AFA8\` border at rest, and \*\*always\*\* a visible affordance of writing: placeholder text in \`#A2AFA8\` (or a label directly above). On focus it morphs (6.5) and the border turns \`#41ED98\`. An input is never an empty silent box. Inputs are the \*\*only tappable element allowed a grey border\*\* — that's what makes them instantly readable as inputs.

\- \*\*Toggle / switch\*\* — never a bare grey square. Off: \`#A2AFA8\` border track, \`#A2AFA8\` square knob, white fill. On: \`#013F17\` fill + \`#013F17\` border, \`#E9FFE1\` square knob at the far end. The knob travels (\`quick\`, ease \`move\`). The toggle's row sits directly on the page with its label and sub-line (4.2) — never wrapped in an extra bordered card.

\- \*\*Checkbox / selectable option\*\* — per Selection (1): off = \`#A2AFA8\` border + icon with \`#012D10\` text; on = \`#41ED98\` border + 5% fill + drawn \`#41ED98\` check.

\- \*\*Stepper (− value +)\*\* — the value is a display-scale hero in \`#012D10\`; − and + are square \`#013F17\`-filled buttons with \`#E9FFE1\` glyphs. No outer container — the size of the number is the container.

\- \*\*Buttons, three tiers\*\* — primary: filled (per section mode); secondary: \`#41ED98\` border-only; tertiary: plain \`#012D10\` bold text with no box ("Pass", "Back", "Post an update instead"). Never present two same-tier buttons side by side — adjacent actions descend the tiers.

\- \*\*Tag\*\* — live/action status keeps the brand treatment: \`#41ED98\` border + 5% fill + \`#41ED98\` text (+ dot for live verbs). Quiet info tags ("Peak was Tuesday, 8–10pm") are filled with no ring — \`#013F17\` default, or \`#95C79D\` / \`#669370\` / \`#5BAA78\` — always with \`#E9FFE1\` text.

\- \*\*Container that truly needs a border\*\* (4.2) — \`#41ED98\` border, never grey. Emphasis containers ("needs you") read brand; inputs read grey; there is no overlap.

\- \*\*Link\*\* — per section-mode link color, underline draw on hover (6.5).

\### 7.2 The texture quota — no monotone screens

A screen made only of white + grey borders + one green button is illegal, even if every element is individually correct. Every screen (and every step of a flow, 5.9) must mix sections and colors tastefully do not overuse the same color

Steps of one flow keep the same skeleton but shift texture with meaning — an input step reads different from a decision step, which reads different from the done-moment. Identical-looking steps make progress feel like standing still.

\### Do

\- Give every role its one signature treatment from the identity table.

\- Cover the copy and check the screen still explains itself.

\- Mix at least three textures per screen, chosen by what things mean.

\- Let inputs be the only grey-bordered tappables on the page.

\- Descend the button tiers when actions sit together.

\### Avoid

\- Two different roles wearing the same border/fill treatment.

\- Toggles or steppers boxed inside bordered rows.

\- Inputs with no placeholder, label, or caret — silent boxes.

\- Screens that are only white + grey borders + one green button.

\- Two same-tier buttons adjacent to each other.

\### Review checklist

\- Every control's role is identifiable with the copy covered.

\- No two roles on the screen share a treatment.

\- Grey borders appear only on inputs at rest.

\- At least three textures from 7.2, each earning its place by meaning.

\- Adjacent actions use descending button tiers.

\- Each flow step is texturally distinct from its neighbors.

\## 8. Screens — two postures, one system

Phone and desktop are not two designs; they are two postures of the same system. Two thresholds do all the work:

\- \*\*900px\*\* — composition: side-by-side moments exist above it; below it they stack in reading order.

\- \*\*600px\*\* — the phone line: borders go 2px → 1px (buttons included, 2), radius 1px → 0.5px, motion durations run ×0.85 (6.1), drawers become sheets, and the dock (8.1) activates.

\### 8.1 Phone — stack, sheet, dock

\- Compositions stack in reading order; whitespace compresses proportionally, never collapses (4.9). Each screenful keeps one focal point.

\- The Explore drawer (5.2) becomes a \*\*bottom sheet\*\*: pinned to the bottom edge, up to ~85% of the screen, padded for the device safe area. Same \`#012D10\`, same content, different posture.

\- \*\*The dock.\*\* The moment's one primary action docks to the bottom edge — fixed, full-width, on solid \`#FAFAFA\` (no gradient fades, 1), padded for the safe area — so the next-best action (5.2) is always under the thumb. The page reserves space beneath it; content never hides behind the dock. On desktop the same action renders as a normal row in its context — the dock is a phone posture, not a component.

\- Every tappable honors the \*\*44px\*\* hard minimum (9).

\- Overlays — toasts, sheets, the dock — respect the safe-area insets; nothing sits under system UI.

\### 8.2 Desktop — anti-timid

\- A stretched phone layout on a big screen is timid and wrong. Compose: text and visual side by side — equal columns, or 3:2 / 2:3 when one side leads — with the wrap breathing to its full width (9).

\- Hover exists only here, so hover only ever enriches (6.5); every affordance and action is complete by tap alone. Nothing is hover-only.

\- The cursor is honest: pointer on everything tappable.

\### Review checklist

\- Below 900px every composition stacks cleanly in reading order.

\- Below 600px: 1px borders, 0.5px radius, ×0.85 durations, sheets instead of side drawers, the dock active.

\- The docked action never covers content, and renders as a normal row on desktop.

\- Every touch target is at least 44px.

\- Nothing depends on hover; desktop layouts use the width instead of stretching the phone design.

\## 9. Sizing — fluid, capped, unbreakable

\*\*Nothing overflows, nothing breaks.\*\* Media never exceeds its container in laptop always visible without scrolling down unless its a text thing like a blog or a text heavy page or a checkout page or any page where the user would expect to scroll down a bit on phone the first glance no buttons should be down like the entire first screen should be visible from the start; text wraps instead of blowing out grid tracks; long values (URLs, ids) truncate with an ellipsis. Text selection highlights in \`#41ED98\` at 15%.
